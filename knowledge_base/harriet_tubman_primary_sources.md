# Harriet Tubman: Primary Source Evidence

## Scope of This Document

This file contains **only primary-source evidence** about Harriet Tubman, organized by topic.
A primary source here means: Tubman's own dictated words; interviews, speeches, or letters from
her lifetime; government records (census, military, pension, deeds); contemporary newspaper accounts;
and documents written by people who personally knew her during her lifetime. Modern historian
interpretation is excluded except brief bracketed notes on source reliability. For scholarly
analysis, gap analysis, and the evidence-confidence matrix, see `harriet_tubman_research.md` and
`harriet_tubman_gap_analysis.md`.

**A note on method:** Tubman was illiterate and left no autobiography in her own hand. The fullest
record of her words is the two biographies she dictated to Sarah Hopkins Bradford — *Scenes in the
Life of Harriet Tubman* (1869) and *Harriet, the Moses of Her People* (1886) — supplemented by
Franklin Sanborn's 1863 and Ednah Dow Cheney's 1865 contemporary sketches, William Still's eyewitness
records, contemporary testimonial letters (Douglass, Seward, Garrett), federal census/pension records,
and a recorded property deed.

**Citation key:** Quotations are followed by an abbreviated reference and, where a verified digital
copy exists, a clickable "View source" link. Page numbers for Bradford 1869 refer to the first
edition (Auburn: W. J. Moses, 1869; Project Gutenberg eBook #57821) and were verified against the
digitized text. Where no link is shown, the source is held in a repository without a verified public
full-text URL (flagged in its source-links line).

## Original Name

### Primary Source — Bradford, *Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman* (1869)

- **Source:** Sarah H. Bradford, *Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman* (recording Tubman's dictation)
- **Author:** Sarah Hopkins Bradford
- **Date:** 1869 (Auburn, N.Y.: W. J. Moses, Printer)
- **Type:** Dictated first-person testimony
- **Repository:** Documenting the American South (UNC-Chapel Hill); Project Gutenberg #57821
- **Page:** 11
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "Then came the call for 'Minty'—Harriet's name was Araminta at the South."

  *— Bradford 1869, p. 11.* [View source](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
@src [Read at DocSouth](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/bradford/bradford.html) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm) | [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/scenesinlifeofha00brad) | [HathiTrust](https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/008686541)

- **Reliability:** First-hand — dictated by Tubman to a biographer who knew her, published in her lifetime.

- **Author:** Franklin B. Sanborn

- **Repository:** Franklin B. Sanborn Papers, American Antiquarian Society, Worcester, Mass.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "Araminta Ross, now known by her married name of Tubman... was born, as near as she can remember, in 1820 or 1821, in Dorchester County, Maryland."

### Primary Source — Brodess Runaway Advertisement, *Cambridge Democrat* (1849)

- **Source:** Eliza Ann Brodess, "Three Hundred Dollars Reward," *Cambridge Democrat* (Cambridge, Md.)
- **Author:** Eliza Ann Brodess (enslaver)
- **Date:** October 3, 1849
- **Type:** Newspaper advertisement / slaveholder's legal notice
- **Repository:** Maryland State Archives; reproduced via Harriet Tubman Monuments project
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "Ranaway from the subscriber on Monday the 17th ult., three negroes, named as follows: Harry, aged about 19 years... Ben, aged about 25 years... Minty, aged about 27 years, is of a chestnut color, fine looking, and about 5 feet high.... Eliza Ann Brodess, Near Bucktown, Dorchester county, Md."

  *— Brodess 1849, Cambridge Democrat.* [View reproduction](https://www.harriettubmanmonuments.slaverymonuments.org/items/show/76)
@src [Reproduction (Tubman Monuments)](https://www.harriettubmanmonuments.slaverymonuments.org/items/show/76) | Repository: Maryland State Archives
- **Analysis:** The enslaver's own advertisement names the escapee as **"Minty,"** independently
  corroborating Tubman's childhood name from a hostile, contemporaneous legal source. It also names
  two brothers (Harry, Ben) and gives her approximate age and description.
- **Reliability:** Contemporary document — created by the slaveholder, hostile witness, high evidentiary value.

### Religious Life / Community

- **Confidence:** Low (no confirmed primary source for formal church membership)
- **Reliability note:** Tubman's deep religious faith is documented throughout her dictated

  through later testimony about visions, prayer, and spirituals.

## Education and Literacy

### Primary Evidence

- **Source:** The form of the surviving record itself — Bradford's *Scenes* (1869) and *Harriet, the
  Moses of Her People* (1886) are **dictated** narratives; Tubman signed documents with an "X" and
  her pension affidavits were recorded by clerks.
- **Type:** Documentary inference from dictated/clerk-recorded sources
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote (Bradford 1869, on how she navigated):**
  > "She had no knowledge of geography, and was ignorant of the conditions of the country through which she was to pass... but she got her directions from the North Star, and from friends met on the way."

  *— Bradford 1869.* [View source](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
@src [Read at DocSouth](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/bradford/bradford.html) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
- **Analysis:** The consistent reliance on dictation and witnessed marks is primary evidence that
  Tubman **could not read or write**. She gathered and remembered information **orally and through
  memory** — by the North Star, songs and spirituals used as coded signals, terrain, and a network of
  trusted contacts — rather than from written maps.
- **Reliability:** Strong inference from the documentary form; **no document in Tubman's own
  handwriting has ever been found** — consistent with illiteracy.

## Marriage

### Primary Source — Marriage to John Tubman

- **Source:** Bradford, *Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman*
- **Author:** Sarah Hopkins Bradford
- **Date:** 1869
- **Type:** Dictated first-person testimony
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "In the year 1844 she was married to a free negro named John Tubman."

  *— Bradford 1869.* [View source](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
@src [Read at DocSouth](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/bradford/bradford.html) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
- **Analysis:** Records her marriage (c. 1844) to **John Tubman**, a free Black man, while she
  remained enslaved. From this marriage she took the surname **Tubman**; combined with adopting her

  **Harriet Tubman**.
- **Reliability:** First-hand.

### Primary Source — Second Marriage (Pension Record)

- **Source:** U.S. pension records / Harriet Tubman Davis pension file, National Archives
- **Date:** Married 1869; pension filings 1890s
- **Type:** Federal government record
- **Repository:** Records of the Veterans Administration, National Archives (NARA), Washington, D.C.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote (substance of record):** Tubman drew a **widow's pension as the widow of Nelson Davis**,
  a private in the 8th United States Colored Infantry, whom she married in 1869.
@src [NARA: Claim of Harriet Tubman](https://www.archives.gov/legislative/features/claim-of-harriet-tubman) | [NARA DocsTeach affidavit](https://www.docsteach.org/documents/document/harriet-tubman-affidavit)

  Tubman's death; the basis of the pension she eventually received.
- **Reliability:** Federal record — high reliability.

## Fugitive Slave Law and Refuge in Canada

### Primary Source — Flight to Canada After 1850

- **Source:** Bradford, *Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman*
- **Author:** Sarah Hopkins Bradford
- **Date:** 1869
- **Type:** Dictated first-person testimony
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "I wouldn't trust Uncle Sam with my people no longer; I brought 'em all clar off to Canada."

  *— Bradford 1869.* [View source](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
@src [Read at DocSouth](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/bradford/bradford.html) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
- **Analysis:** After the **Fugitive Slave Act of 1850** made capture possible even in free Northern
  states, Tubman extended her routes to **Canada (Canada West / Ontario)**. By her own testimony she
  ceased stopping in the Northern U.S. and conducted fugitives "clear off to Canada."
- **Reliability:** First-hand.

### Primary Source — St. Catharines Residence (Census / Local Records)

- **Source:** Canadian census and St. Catharines municipal/church records; Fugitive Aid Society records
- **Date:** 1851–1861

- **Repository:** Library and Archives Canada; St. Catharines Museum
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote (substance):** Tubman **rented a house on North Street, St. Catharines, Canada West**, from
  December 1851, using it as a base between rescue missions; in 1861 she helped form the **Fugitive

@src [Library and Archives Canada](https://library-archives.canada.ca/eng/) | [LOC research guide](https://guides.loc.gov/harriet-tubman)

  Slave Law era and her organizational work supporting other refugees.
- **Reliability:** Contemporary records — high; [some details synthesized by local historians].

## Underground Railroad

### Primary Source — William Still's Eyewitness Records

- **Source:** William Still, *The Underground Railroad*
- **Author:** William Still
- **Date:** 1872 (Philadelphia: Porter & Coates)
- **Type:** Contemporary record by the Philadelphia Vigilance Committee secretary who personally
  received fugitives Tubman conducted
- **Repository:** Library Company of Philadelphia; Internet Archive; Project Gutenberg #15263
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "Harriet Tubman had been their 'Moses,'... She had faithfully gone down into Egypt, and had delivered these... by her own heroism."

  *— Still 1872.* [View source](https://archive.org/details/undergroundrailr00stil)
@src [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/undergroundrailr00stil) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/15263)
- **Analysis:** Still, who kept meticulous contemporaneous records of escapees passing through
  Philadelphia, documents Tubman as an active **conductor** bringing groups north — a critical
  independent, non-Bradford primary source.
- **Reliability:** Contemporary witness with documentary records — very high.

- **Source:** Thomas Garrett (Quaker abolitionist, Wilmington, Delaware), letters/recollections
  printed in Bradford (1869)
- **Author:** Thomas Garrett
- **Date:** 1860s
- **Type:** Letter / statement by a contemporary who personally aided her
- **Repository:** Reprinted in Bradford 1869 (DocSouth; Project Gutenberg #57821)
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "I never met with any person of any color who had more confidence in the voice of God, as spoken direct to her soul."

@src [Read at DocSouth](https://docsouth.unc.edu/neh/bradford/bradford.html) | [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57821/57821-h/57821-h.htm)
- **Analysis:** Garrett, who sheltered and funded Tubman's parties at Wilmington, corroborates both

## Civil War Service

### Primary Source — Tubman's Pension Affidavit

- **Source:** Affidavit of Harriet Tubman (Davis), pension/claim file, Records of the U.S. House of
  Representatives, National Archives
- **Author:** Harriet Tubman (Davis), sworn
- **Date:** ca. 1898
- **Type:** Federal government record / sworn affidavit
- **Repository:** Records of the U.S. House of Representatives, NARA; digitized at DocsTeach
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "My claim against the U.S. is for three years' services as nurse and cook in hospitals, and as commander of several men (eight or nine) as scouts during the late war of the Rebellion."

  *— Tubman affidavit, ca. 1898 (NARA).* [View source](https://www.docsteach.org/documents/document/harriet-tubman-affidavit)
@src [NARA DocsTeach (affidavit)](https://www.docsteach.org/documents/document/harriet-tubman-affidavit) | [NARA: Claim of Harriet Tubman](https://www.archives.gov/legislative/features/claim-of-harriet-tubman)
- **Analysis:** In her **own sworn claim**, Tubman documents service as **nurse, cook, scout, and
  commander of a scouting party** for the Union Army — primary evidence of nursing, scouting, and
  spying roles.
- **Reliability:** Sworn federal record — very high.

### Primary Source — Combahee River Raid (Contemporary Newspaper)

- **Source:** *Wisconsin State Journal* (and reprinted/echoed in *The Commonwealth*, Boston)
- **Date:** July 1863
- **Type:** Contemporary newspaper account
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote:**
  > "A gallant band of 300 black soldiers, under the guidance of a black woman, dashed into the enemy's country... and brought off near 800 slaves and thousands of dollars' worth of property, without losing a man or receiving a scratch."

  *— Wisconsin State Journal, July 1863.*
@src Repository: contemporary newspaper — exact wording from transcriptions; no verified free full-text URL located. [NPS: Combahee context](https://www.nps.gov/articles/harriet-tubman-and-the-54th-massachusetts.htm)
- **Analysis:** Contemporary press confirms the **Combahee River Raid (June 2, 1863)** led by Col.
  James Montgomery and **guided by Tubman** ("a black woman"), freeing roughly **700–800 enslaved
  people** — the first U.S. military operation planned and led with a woman's guidance.
- **Reliability:** Contemporary document. [Reliability note: exact wording is reproduced from
  reputable transcriptions and not yet checked against an original page scan; Tubman unnamed in the
  raid report but identified shortly after by Sanborn in *The Commonwealth*.]

### Primary Source — Pension Outcome (Government Record)

- **Source:** U.S. pension records; Act of Congress / pension increase approved 1899
- **Date:** 1899
- **Type:** Federal record
- **Repository:** Records of the Veterans Administration, NARA
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote (substance):** Tubman's widow's pension (as widow of Nelson Davis) was **raised from $8 to
  $20 per month** in 1899; Congress declined to grant a separate pension for her own military service.
@src [NARA: Claim of Harriet Tubman](https://www.archives.gov/legislative/features/claim-of-harriet-tubman)
- **Analysis:** Government record of partial compensation; documents official acknowledgment of her
  service while underscoring that her direct military service was never fully compensated.
- **Reliability:** Federal record — high.

## Property Ownership and Financial Life

### Primary Source — The 1859 Seward Deed

- **Source:** Property deed, Frances A. Seward to Harriet Tubman, Cayuga County (Fleming/Auburn), N.Y.
- **Author:** Frances A. Seward / Cayuga County Clerk
- **Date:** 1859
- **Type:** Recorded real-property deed (government record)
- **Repository:** Cayuga County land records, Auburn, N.Y.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Quote (substance):** Tubman purchased **seven acres and a house on South Street, Fleming, N.Y.**,
  from **Frances Adeline Seward** (wife of William H. Seward) for **$1,200**, paying $25 down and $10
  per quarter on a privately held mortgage.
@src Repository: Cayuga County land records, Auburn, N.Y. — offline (no public URL). [NPS: Harriet Tubman NHP](https://www.nps.gov/hart/index.htm)
- **Analysis:** A recorded deed documenting Tubman's **property ownership** — extraordinary for a
  Black woman and a fugitive in 1859 — establishing her lifelong Auburn home base.
- **Reliability:** Government land record — very high.

### Primary Source — Financial Hardship and the 1873 Swindle

- **Source:** Contemporary newspaper accounts, central New York, 1873
- **Date:** 1873
- **Type:** Contemporary newspaper reports
- **Confidence:** Medium
- **Quote (substance):** Tubman was **defrauded in a "gold swindle,"** lured by con men promising a
  cache of Confederate gold; she was robbed of roughly $2,000 she had helped raise, reported in the
  regional press.
@src Repository: contemporary newspapers — no verified free full-text URL located.
- **Analysis:** Documents her chronic **financial hardship** — she lived near poverty, supported
  many dependents, and waited decades for a pension. [Reliability note: the swindle is reported in
  contemporary newspapers and recounted by Bradford; specific dollar figures vary by account.]
- **Reliability:** Contemporary press — medium-high (figures vary).
