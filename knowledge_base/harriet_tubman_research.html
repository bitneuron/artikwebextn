Executive Summary#

Harriet Tubman (born Araminta "Minty" Ross, c. 1820–1822 – died March 10, 1913) was an enslaved woman from Maryland's Eastern Shore who escaped to freedom in 1849 and became the most famous conductor of the Underground Railroad, the first woman to guide a U.S. armed military raid, and a lifelong activist for emancipation, the aged poor, and women's suffrage. Born into slavery in Dorchester County to Benjamin Ross and Harriet "Rit" Greene, she endured childhood separation, hired-out labor, physical abuse, and a traumatic head injury that produced lifelong "sleeping spells" and the religious visions she regarded as divine guidance. After her own escape, she returned to the Eastern Shore roughly thirteen times, personally leading some 70 people to freedom and instructing dozens more — by her own account never losing a passenger. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 pushed her routes all the way to Canada (St. Catharines, Ontario), where she based herself for a decade. During the Civil War she served the Union Army as nurse, cook, scout, and spy, and guided the Combahee River Raid of June 2, 1863, which freed roughly 700–800 enslaved people. She spent her later decades in Auburn, New York — where she had purchased property from the Sewards in 1859 — founding the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged, working with the AME Zion Church, supporting women's suffrage, and enduring chronic poverty until her death.

Part I – Primary Sources#

Primary source = Tubman's own dictated words; lifetime interviews, speeches, and letters; government records; contemporary newspapers; and documents by people who knew her in her lifetime. Citation key: each quote is attributed to its source and year; page numbers for Bradford 1869 (verified against Project Gutenberg #57821) appear in the companion file harriet_tubman_primary_sources.md, which is the canonical, fully-cited quote archive.

Original Name#

Araminta Ross · "Minty" · Harriet Tubman · name-change evidence

Source: Sarah H. Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman — Date: 1869 — Type: Dictated testimony
Quote: "Then came the call for 'Minty'—Harriet's name was Araminta at the South."
Analysis: Birth name Araminta, called Minty. She later took Harriet (her mother's name) and Tubman (her 1844 marriage). Surname Ross from her father.
Reliability: First-hand.

Source: Franklin B. Sanborn, The Commonwealth — Date: July 17, 1863 — Type: Newspaper
Quote: "Araminta Ross, now known by her married name of Tubman..."
Reliability: Contemporary witness.

Source: Eliza Ann Brodess, Cambridge Democrat — Date: Oct. 3, 1849 — Type: Slaveholder's ad
Quote: "Minty, aged about 27 years, is of a chestnut color, fine looking, and about 5 feet high."
Reliability: Contemporary document, hostile-witness corroboration.

Family Background#

Benjamin Ross · Harriet "Rit" Greene · siblings · extended family · African ancestry

Source: Bradford 1869 — Type: Dictated testimony
Quote: "Her parents were Benjamin Ross and Harriet Greene, both slaves, but married and faithful to each other."
Quote: "Frequently Harriet worked for her father, who was a timber inspector..."
Quote: "She had seen two older sisters carried off in a chain-gang to the far South."
Analysis: Parents named and confirmed enslaved; father a skilled timber inspector; older sisters sold south — a trauma that shaped her rescues.
Reliability: First-hand.

Source: Brodess ad (1849) — names brothers "Harry" and "Ben." Reliability: Contemporary document.

Source: Sanborn 1863 — "the grand-daughter of a slave imported from Africa..." (oral tradition of African descent). Reliability: Contemporary witness reporting tradition.

Childhood#

Birthplace · living conditions · childhood labor · separation · abuse · religion · resistance

Source: Bradford 1869 — Type: Dictated testimony
Quote: "She was born, as near as she can remember, in 1820 or in 1821, in Dorchester County, on the Eastern shore of Maryland, and not far from the town of Cambridge."
Quote: "When Harriet was six years old, she was taken from her mother and carried ten miles to live with James Cook... Cook set her to watching his musk-rat traps, which compelled her to wade through the water."
Quote: "...down would come the whip upon the neck and face of the poor weary creature."
Analysis: Birthplace Dorchester Co.; separation at six; hired-out child labor; physical abuse; lifelong scars. Earliest resistance = refusing to bind a fleeing enslaved man.
Religion/community: Faith attested via later visions/prayer testimony. No confirmed primary source describing formal childhood church membership has been located.
Reliability: First-hand.

Education and Literacy#

  • Could she read? Primary evidence indicates no. Every record of her words is dictated; she used an "X" mark; no document in her handwriting exists.
  • Could she write? No confirmed primary evidence; no autograph document found.
  • How she learned/communicated: Orally and from memory — the North Star, terrain, spirituals used as coded signals, and a trusted human network.
  • Primary support (Bradford 1869): "She had no knowledge of geography... but she got her directions from the North Star, and from friends met on the way."
  • Reliability: Strong documentary inference; No confirmed primary source shows Tubman reading or writing.

Head Injury#

Source: Bradford 1869 — Type: Dictated testimony
Quote: "The overseer caught up a two-pound weight from the counter and threw it at the fugitive, but it fell short and struck Harriet a stunning blow on the head."
Quote: "From that time she was subject to a sort of stupor or lethargy at times... throwing her into a deep sleep, from which she would presently rouse herself."
Quote: "She seemed to have visions of the future, and would tell of various peculiar and prophetic dreams."
Analysis: Iron-weight injury in adolescence → lifelong sleeping spells and religious visions she read as divine guidance.
Reliability: First-hand; corroborated by contemporaries.

Marriage#

Source: Bradford 1869 — Quote: "In the year 1844 she was married to a free negro named John Tubman."
Analysis: Marriage to free Black man John Tubman (~1844); origin of surname Tubman; combined with adopting her mother's name Harriet = change from Araminta Ross to Harriet Tubman.
Source: Pension file (NARA) — Married Nelson Davis (1869), a USCT veteran; basis of widow's pension.
Reliability: First-hand / federal record.

Escape from Slavery#

Why: Threat of being sold south after her enslaver's death.
Source: Bradford 1886 — Quote: "there was one of two things I had a right to, liberty or death; if I could not have one, I would have the other."
Timeline: Brodess ad (Cambridge Democrat, Oct 3, 1849) dates flight to Sept 17, 1849.
Arrival: Bradford 1869 — "When I found I had crossed that line... There was such a glory over everything... I felt like I was in Heaven." (Pennsylvania, 1849.)
Reliability: First-hand + hostile-witness corroboration.

Fugitive Slave Law and Refuge in Canada#

Source: Bradford 1869 — Type: Dictated testimony
Quote: "I wouldn't trust Uncle Sam with my people no longer; I brought 'em all clar off to Canada."
Analysis: The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 made free-state capture possible, so Tubman extended routes to Canada West (Ontario). She based herself at St. Catharines (North Street) from December 1851 to ~1861 and co-founded the Fugitive Aid Society there (1861) with her brother William Henry.
Source: Canadian census / St. Catharines records — Reliability: Contemporary records, high.

Underground Railroad#

Source: William Still, The Underground Railroad (1872) — Type: Eyewitness records
Quote: "Harriet Tubman had been their 'Moses,'... She had faithfully gone down into Egypt, and had delivered these... by her own heroism."
Source: Thomas Garrett (in Bradford 1869) — Quote: "I never met with any person of any color who had more confidence in the voice of God, as spoken direct to her soul."
Source: Frederick Douglass, letter Aug 29, 1868 (in Bradford 1869) — Quote: "Excepting John Brown—of sacred memory—I know of no one who has willingly encountered more perils and hardships to serve our enslaved people than you have."
Source: Ednah Dow Cheney, The Freedmen's Record (March 1865) — Quote: "She is probably the most remarkable woman of this age."
Her own summary (1896): "I never ran my train off the track and I never lost a passenger."
Reliability: Multiple independent contemporary witnesses — very high.

Civil War Service#

Source: Affidavit of Harriet Tubman (Davis), pension/claim file, NARA — Date: ca. 1898
Quote: "My claim against the U.S. is for three years' services as nurse and cook in hospitals, and as commander of several men (eight or nine) as scouts during the late war of the Rebellion."
Source: Wisconsin State Journal / The Commonwealth (July 1863) — Quote: "...under the guidance of a black woman... brought off near 800 slaves... without losing a man or receiving a scratch."
Analysis: Sworn record of nursing, cooking, scouting, and commanding scouts; contemporary press confirms the Combahee River Raid (June 2, 1863) she guided.
Source: U.S. pension records (1899) — widow's pension raised $8 → $20/month; no separate pension for her own service.
Reliability: Sworn federal record + contemporary press — very high.

Reconstruction and Later Life#

Auburn property · financial hardship · Tubman Home · church · suffrage · death

Source: Bradford 1869 + 1859 Seward deed — Tubman bought 7 acres in Fleming/Auburn from Frances Seward for $1,200 (1859), her lifelong base. Seward: "I have known her long, and a nobler, higher spirit... seldom dwells in the human form."
Financial hardship: Defrauded in an 1873 "gold swindle" (contemporary press); lived near poverty supporting many dependents.
Caregiving / Tubman Home: Bought 25 adjoining acres (1896); founded the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged; deeded it to the AME Zion Church (1903); Home opened 1908.
Suffrage/activism: Spoke at women's suffrage gatherings (with Susan B. Anthony) and the 1896 NACW founding meeting.
Death/burial: Died of pneumonia March 10, 1913, Auburn; buried with semi-military honors at
Fort Hill Cemetery. (Death certificate's 1815 and gravestone's 1820 birth years are unreliable.)
Reliability: Deeds/vital records high; swindle figures medium.

Part II – Secondary Sources#

Secondary source = analysis by modern historians and institutions. Presented after the primary evidence above and kept separate from it.

Original Name & Birth Year#

Citation: Kate Clifford Larson, Bound for the Promised Land: Harriet Tubman, Portrait of an American Hero (New York: Ballantine Books, 2004).
Key Argument: Tubman was born Araminta Ross in early 1822 (Feb–Mar), not 1820/21.
Evidence Used: A midwife/doctor payment recorded in Edward Brodess's accounts (a primary record Larson located), plus the 1849 runaway ad and other documents.
Strengths: Anchors the birth year to a contemporaneous financial record rather than memory.
Limitations: The payment infers, rather than states, the birth; competing records (1815, 1820, 1825) remain.

Citation: Catherine Clinton, Harriet Tubman: The Road to Freedom (New York: Little, Brown, 2004).
Key Argument: Weighs multiple candidate birth dates; emphasizes documentary uncertainty.
Evidence Used: Census, estate, and pension records; Bradford read critically.
Strengths: Transparent about conflicting evidence.
Limitations: Less decisive on a single date than Larson.

Family, Genealogy & Childhood#

Citation: Larson, Bound for the Promised Land (2004).
Key Argument: Reconstructs the Ross family under the Brodess/Thompson households near the Blackwater River; identifies nine children of Ben and Rit, including sisters Linah, Mariah Ritty, and Soph (sold south) and brothers Robert, Ben, Henry, Moses, and William Henry.
Evidence Used: Maryland land, estate, tax, and probate records.
Strengths: Deep archival grounding; recovers a family tree absent from the primary narratives.
Limitations: Some kinship links inferential given gaps in slaveholder records.

Citation: National Park Service, Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad National Historical Park.
Key Argument: Synthesizes the documented arc of her Eastern Shore childhood and rescues.
Strengths: Authoritative, vetted public history. Limitations: Summary level.

Head Injury, Visions & Health#

Citation: Larson (2004); Clinton (2004).
Key Argument: The skull injury likely caused temporal lobe epilepsy / narcolepsy, explaining the sleeping spells and the visions she experienced as religious revelation.
Evidence Used: Bradford's symptom descriptions read against modern neurology.
Strengths: Plausible framing consistent with primary symptoms. Limitations: Retrospective; unprovable.

Underground Railroad (incl. numbers myth)#

Citation: Larson, Bound for the Promised Land (2004).
Key Argument: Tubman made roughly 13 rescue missions and personally freed about 70 people, instructing ~70 more — far below the legendary "300+."
Evidence Used: Still's records, Garrett's letters, network testimony, dated escapes.
Strengths: Corrects mythologized numbers. Limitations: Some missions sparsely documented.

Citation: Jean M. Humez, Harriet Tubman: The Life and the Life Stories (Madison: Univ. of Wisconsin Press, 2003).
Key Argument: Analyzes how Tubman's story was shaped by Bradford and other transcribers; distinguishes Tubman's voice from her editors'.
Strengths: Essential source-criticism; compiles printed primary recollections.
Limitations: Interpretive/literary rather than new archival discovery.

Civil War Service#

Citation: Clinton (2004); Library of Congress and National Archives educational materials.
Key Argument: Her wartime nursing, scouting, and spying were real and significant, yet the federal government under-recognized and under-compensated her for decades.
Evidence Used: NARA pension/claim file, congressional records, Combahee press.
Strengths: Ties primary documents to the politics of recognition. Limitations: Spying details thin.

Later Life, Memory & Legacy#

Citation: Beverly Lowry, Harriet Tubman: Imagining a Life (New York: Doubleday, 2007).
Key Argument: Narrative treatment of her later activism, the Auburn home, and symbolic legacy.
Strengths: Accessible; attentive to memory-making. Limitations: More literary reconstruction.

Citation: Milton C. Sernett, Harriet Tubman: Myth, Memory, and History (Durham: Duke Univ. Press, 2007).
Key Argument: Distinguishes the symbolic "Tubman" of memory from the historical figure; traces how Bradford, Conrad, and later writers shaped (and exaggerated) the legend.
Evidence Used: Historiographical analysis of all major biographies and commemorations.
Strengths: The definitive study of Tubman's memory and myth-making.
Limitations: Historiography, not a life narrative; assumes familiarity with the basic story.

Citation: Earl Conrad, Harriet Tubman (Washington, D.C.: Associated Publishers, 1943).
Key Argument: First modern, research-based biography; recovered documents and contemporaries' testimony decades after Bradford.
Strengths: Pioneering archival recovery; bridged the 19th-century sources to modern scholarship.
Limitations: Shaped by the author's politics; superseded on details by Larson/Clinton.

Part VI – Historical Context#

Background syntheses (drawn from the secondary literature and institutional sources) that situate the primary evidence. These are interpretive context, not primary testimony.

Slavery on Maryland's Eastern Shore#

Dorchester County was a tidewater region of mixed agriculture (grain, not just tobacco) and extensive timbering and waterway work. Enslaved people were frequently hired out to other farms, and labor needs created a distinctive pattern of family separation across plantations — exactly the world the primary sources show for the Ross family.

Free Black Communities and Mixed Families#

The Eastern Shore had an unusually large free Black population living alongside the enslaved; marriages like Rit (enslaved) and arrangements like Ben Ross's later manumission, plus Harriet's marriage to the free John Tubman, were common. This proximity of slavery and freedom shaped escape networks and kin ties.

The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850#

Part of the Compromise of 1850, the law compelled Northern officials and citizens to assist in recapturing escapees and stripped the accused of due process. It made the free North unsafe, pushing the Underground Railroad's terminus to Canada — the direct cause of Tubman's St. Catharines years.

Underground Railroad Networks#

Not a single organization but a decentralized web of safe houses and agents (Quakers like Thomas Garrett, vigilance committees like William Still's in Philadelphia, Black churches, and Canadian aid societies). Tubman operated within and helped knit this network, using spirituals as coded signals.

Civil War Military Operations in the Low Country#

The Union's 1862–63 occupation of the South Carolina Sea Islands (the "Port Royal Experiment") created conditions for Black enlistment and operations like the Combahee River Raid, in which gunboats ascended a tidal river to destroy plantations and liberate the enslaved — Tubman providing intelligence.

The Women's Suffrage Movement#

In her later decades Tubman lent her prestige to the woman-suffrage cause, appearing with figures like Susan B. Anthony and at the 1896 founding of the National Association of Colored Women, linking abolition's legacy to the next generation's struggle for rights.
