Scope of This Document#

This file contains only primary-source evidence about Harriet Tubman, organized by topic. A primary source here means: Tubman's own dictated words; interviews, speeches, or letters from her lifetime; government records (census, military, pension, deeds); contemporary newspaper accounts; and documents written by people who personally knew her during her lifetime. Modern historian interpretation is excluded except brief bracketed notes on source reliability. For scholarly analysis, gap analysis, and the evidence-confidence matrix, see harriet_tubman_research.md and harriet_tubman_gap_analysis.md.

A note on method: Tubman was illiterate and left no autobiography in her own hand. The fullest record of her words is the two biographies she dictated to Sarah Hopkins Bradford — Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman (1869) and Harriet, the Moses of Her People (1886) — supplemented by Franklin Sanborn's 1863 and Ednah Dow Cheney's 1865 contemporary sketches, William Still's eyewitness records, contemporary testimonial letters (Douglass, Seward, Garrett), federal census/pension records, and a recorded property deed.

Citation key: Quotations are followed by an abbreviated reference and, where a verified digital copy exists, a clickable "View source" link. Page numbers for Bradford 1869 refer to the first edition (Auburn: W. J. Moses, 1869; Project Gutenberg eBook #57821) and were verified against the digitized text. Where no link is shown, the source is held in a repository without a verified public full-text URL (flagged in its source-links line).

Original Name#

Primary Source — Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman (1869)#

  • Source: Sarah H. Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman (recording Tubman's dictation)
  • Author: Sarah Hopkins Bradford
  • Date: 1869 (Auburn, N.Y.: W. J. Moses, Printer)
  • Type: Dictated first-person testimony
  • Repository: Documenting the American South (UNC-Chapel Hill); Project Gutenberg #57821
  • Page: 11
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"Then came the call for 'Minty'—Harriet's name was Araminta at the South."

— Bradford 1869, p. 11. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg | Internet Archive | HathiTrust
  • Analysis: Establishes that the woman publicly known as "Harriet" was named Araminta in enslaved childhood, called "Minty." The surname Ross came from her father (see Family Background). She later adopted "Harriet" (her mother's name) by adulthood.
  • Reliability: First-hand — dictated by Tubman to a biographer who knew her, published in her lifetime.

Primary Source — Sanborn, The Commonwealth (1863)#

  • Source: Franklin B. Sanborn, "Harriet Tubman," The Commonwealth (Boston)
  • Author: Franklin B. Sanborn
  • Date: July 17, 1863
  • Type: Contemporary newspaper account by a man who knew and interviewed her
  • Repository: Franklin B. Sanborn Papers, American Antiquarian Society, Worcester, Mass.
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"Araminta Ross, now known by her married name of Tubman... was born, as near as she can remember, in 1820 or 1821, in Dorchester County, Maryland."

— Sanborn 1863, The Commonwealth.

Repository: Sanborn Papers, American Antiquarian Society — no verified public full-text URL located. LOC research guide
  • Analysis: The earliest known printed source naming Tubman; independently records her birth name as Araminta Ross. Confirms the name evidence six years before Bradford's book.
  • Reliability: Contemporary document / contemporary witness.

Primary Source — Brodess Runaway Advertisement, Cambridge Democrat (1849)#

  • Source: Eliza Ann Brodess, "Three Hundred Dollars Reward," Cambridge Democrat (Cambridge, Md.)
  • Author: Eliza Ann Brodess (enslaver)
  • Date: October 3, 1849
  • Type: Newspaper advertisement / slaveholder's legal notice
  • Repository: Maryland State Archives; reproduced via Harriet Tubman Monuments project
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"Ranaway from the subscriber on Monday the 17th ult., three negroes, named as follows: Harry, aged about 19 years... Ben, aged about 25 years... Minty, aged about 27 years, is of a chestnut color, fine looking, and about 5 feet high.... Eliza Ann Brodess, Near Bucktown, Dorchester county, Md."

— Brodess 1849, Cambridge Democrat. View reproduction

Reproduction (Tubman Monuments) | Repository: Maryland State Archives
  • Analysis: The enslaver's own advertisement names the escapee as "Minty," independently corroborating Tubman's childhood name from a hostile, contemporaneous legal source. It also names two brothers (Harry, Ben) and gives her approximate age and description.
  • Reliability: Contemporary document — created by the slaveholder, hostile witness, high evidentiary value.

Religious Life / Community#

  • Confidence: Low (no confirmed primary source for formal church membership)
  • Reliability note: Tubman's deep religious faith is documented throughout her dictated narratives and by contemporaries (see Head Injury visions; Thomas Garrett under Underground Railroad). Her mother told her Bible stories orally. No confirmed standalone primary source describing formal childhood church membership has been located; her faith is attested mainly through later testimony about visions, prayer, and spirituals.

Education and Literacy#

Primary Evidence#

  • Source: The form of the surviving record itself — Bradford's Scenes (1869) and Harriet, the Moses of Her People (1886) are dictated narratives; Tubman signed documents with an "X" and her pension affidavits were recorded by clerks.
  • Type: Documentary inference from dictated/clerk-recorded sources
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote (Bradford 1869, on how she navigated):
"She had no knowledge of geography, and was ignorant of the conditions of the country through which she was to pass... but she got her directions from the North Star, and from friends met on the way."

— Bradford 1869. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg
  • Analysis: The consistent reliance on dictation and witnessed marks is primary evidence that Tubman could not read or write. She gathered and remembered information orally and through memory — by the North Star, songs and spirituals used as coded signals, terrain, and a network of trusted contacts — rather than from written maps.
  • Reliability: Strong inference from the documentary form; no document in Tubman's own handwriting has ever been found — consistent with illiteracy.

Marriage#

  • Source: Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman
  • Author: Sarah Hopkins Bradford
  • Date: 1869
  • Type: Dictated first-person testimony
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"In the year 1844 she was married to a free negro named John Tubman."

— Bradford 1869. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg
  • Analysis: Records her marriage (c. 1844) to John Tubman, a free Black man, while she remained enslaved. From this marriage she took the surname Tubman; combined with adopting her mother's name Harriet, this is the documented basis of the change from Araminta Ross to Harriet Tubman.

Fugitive Slave Law and Refuge in Canada#

Primary Source — Flight to Canada After 1850#

  • Source: Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman
  • Author: Sarah Hopkins Bradford
  • Date: 1869
  • Type: Dictated first-person testimony
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"I wouldn't trust Uncle Sam with my people no longer; I brought 'em all clar off to Canada."

— Bradford 1869. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg
  • Analysis: After the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 made capture possible even in free Northern states, Tubman extended her routes to Canada (Canada West / Ontario). By her own testimony she ceased stopping in the Northern U.S. and conducted fugitives "clear off to Canada."
  • Reliability: First-hand.

Later Life#

Primary Source — Auburn Property from Seward (Testimony)#

  • Source: Bradford, Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman
  • Author: Sarah Hopkins Bradford
  • Date: 1869
  • Type: Dictated first-person testimony / contemporary statement
  • Repository: DocSouth; Project Gutenberg #57821
  • Page: 72
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"a little property at Auburn, N. Y., which their daughter purchased for them from Mr. Seward, the Secretary of State."

— Bradford 1869, p. 72. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg
  • Analysis: Documents Tubman's Auburn, New York home (see also the 1859 deed above), where she settled her family and lived the rest of her life.
  • Reliability: First-hand / contemporary; corroborated by the recorded deed and Seward's testimonial.

Primary Source — Seward's Testimonial#

  • Source: William H. Seward, statement printed in Bradford (1869)
  • Author: William H. Seward
  • Date: 1860s
  • Type: Statement by a contemporary who knew her and sold her the property
  • Repository: Reprinted in Bradford 1869
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote:
"I have known her long, and a nobler, higher spirit, or a truer, seldom dwells in the human form."

— Seward, in Bradford 1869. View source

Read at DocSouth | Project Gutenberg
  • Analysis: Confirms the Seward–Tubman relationship and the Auburn connection from the seller's side.
  • Reliability: Contemporary witness — high.

Primary Source — The Tubman Home and Church (Institutional Records)#

  • Source: Records of the AME Zion Church and the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged, Auburn, N.Y.
  • Date: Adjacent 25 acres bought 1896; property deeded to AME Zion Church 1903; Home opened 1908
  • Type: Institutional/church records and deeds from her lifetime
  • Repository: AME Zion Church; Harriet Tubman Home; Cayuga County records
  • Confidence: High
  • Quote (substance): Tubman bought an adjoining 25-acre parcel at auction in 1896, established the Harriet Tubman Home for Aged and Indigent Colored People, and deeded it to the AME Zion Church (1903), which opened the Home in 1908.
NPS: Harriet Tubman Home | NPS: Harriet Tubman NHP
  • Analysis: Documents her church involvement and charitable caregiving work in later life.
  • Reliability: Institutional records / deeds — high.

Primary Source — Women's Suffrage#

  • Source: Contemporary reports of suffrage gatherings; Tubman's reported remarks (1896)
  • Date: 1890s
  • Type: Reported speech / contemporary account
  • Confidence: Medium-High
  • Quote (substance): Tubman appeared at women's suffrage meetings, spoke alongside Susan B. Anthony, and attended the 1896 founding meeting of the National Association of Colored Women.
LOC: Harriet Tubman research guide
  • Analysis: Evidence of her later activism linking abolition's legacy to the suffrage cause.
  • Reliability: Reported contemporaneous account.

Primary Source — Death and Burial#

  • Source: Death certificate and contemporary obituaries; gravestone, Fort Hill Cemetery, Auburn, N.Y.
  • Date: March 10, 1913
  • Type: Government vital record / contemporary newspaper / monument
  • Repository: New York State vital records; Fort Hill Cemetery, Auburn
  • Confidence: High (death) / Low (retroactive birth year)
  • Quote (substance): Tubman died of pneumonia at the Tubman Home on March 10, 1913, and was buried with semi-military honors at Fort Hill Cemetery, Auburn.
LOC: digital resources | NPS: Harriet Tubman NHP
  • Analysis: Vital records and obituaries document her death and burial. [Reliability note: the death certificate lists a birth year of 1815 and the gravestone 1820 — neither matches her own or Larson's estimates, illustrating the unreliability of late records for her birth date.]
  • Reliability: Government vital record / contemporary press — high for death, low for the retroactively stated birth year.
